Discipline Knowledge & Growth

My Mid-Term Study Strategy

For my mid-terms, which covered lectures 2, 3, 4, 5, and 7, I didn't just try to memorize facts. My method was to first gather all my notes and the lecture slides into one place. Then, I focused on understanding the *real meaning* of the topics and how they connect. For example, I made charts to link ideas from different weeks, like how 'Deviant Online Behavior' (Week 4) and 'Personality in Cyberspace' (Week 5) both build on the ideas of 'Online Identity' (Week 2). This way, I actually *learned* the material for the long run, not just for the test.

Domain Reflections & Professional Development

Domain 1: Abnormal Psychology & Cyber Behavior

This area (from Weeks 4 & 5) covered *why* people act the way they do online. We learned about different online personalities, what motives cybercriminals have, and deviant behavior.

Reflection:

This is critical for my career. Security isn't just about computers; it's about people. Knowing *why* someone might launch an attack or how to spot manipulation helps me understand **social engineering**. Instead of just blocking a bad IP address, I can think about the *person* behind the attack and what they're after. This helps build much smarter defenses.

Domain 2: Thinking & Artificial Intelligence

These lectures (from Weeks 9 & 10) were about how *we* think, solve problems, and how our brains store information. We then compared this to how Artificial Intelligence (AI) works.

Reflection:

This helps me in two ways. First, many modern security tools (like threat detectors) use AI. By knowing *how* AI 'thinks,' I can use those tools better and not just trust them blindly. Second, learning about human problem-solving helps me improve my *own* skills. When there's a security alert, I know how to think through the problem step-by-step to find the real cause, rather than jumping to the wrong conclusion.

Inquiry & Critical Thinking

My classes, especially on cyber behavior, have taught me to be a better critical thinker. I've learned that a person's online profile isn't just a profile; it's a collection of clues about their personality and habits, which can be used in attacks. This understanding goes beyond just the technical side and focuses on the human element—which is often the weakest link.

In my future job, this means I'll have a clear process for investigating problems. When a security alarm goes off, my first step is to brainstorm all the possibilities (e.g., "Is this malware? An insider threat? A false alarm?"). After I have all the ideas, I'll use logic to find the *one* right answer. This stops me from getting stuck on the first or most obvious solution, which is often the wrong one, and helps me find the real root of the problem.
